How it works

What the Worn Wings program does

When you bring us a bird, we assess its condition, document the cause of harm, and begin a course of treatment. Our volunteer rehabilitators — trained in avian triage, wound care, and nutritional recovery — will nurse the bird back to health and return it to its territory. The whole process takes, on average, four to eight weeks.

We ask one thing of you in return: tell us where and how you found the bird. Every intake form is a data point. We are building the evidence base that this city refuses to compile itself.

01 · Find

A bird in distress. Look for signs: inability to fly, visible wound, lethargic behaviour, entangled leg band, trembling.

02 · Contain

Use a cardboard box with air holes. Do not offer water or food — well-meaning feeding can cause aspiration in injured birds.

03 · Submit

Fill out the intake form on this page or drop in to one of our three intake locations across the boroughs.

04 · Return

You'll receive updates. When the bird is ready, we release it at or near its home territory.

"You cannot protect what you have been taught to despise. Change the story, and everything else becomes possible."

What we treat

The injuries are not random

Every wound we treat is traceable back to a policy choice. Spike strips are a planning decision. Contaminated grain is a procurement decision. Leg bands left until they cut through flesh are an enforcement failure. We treat the injury. We name the cause.

  • Spike-related lacerations and wing injuries — treatable in most cases
  • Leg-band constriction and necrosis — early intervention critical
  • Netting entanglement — cuts off circulation; time-sensitive
  • Malnutrition from laced grain — supportive care, variable outcomes
  • Secondary poisoning from pest control — supportive care, variable outcomes
  • Severe internal injuries from impact — assessed case by case; some beyond our capacity
  • Advanced aspergillosis — palliative care only; refer to specialist
About repair

Repaired birds may not look like new.

We will always aim to restore full function. But a bird that has lost a toe to a leg band, or bears scar tissue from a spike wound, carries that history in its body. We do not consider this a failure. We consider it evidence. These birds return to the city marked by what the city did to them — and they return anyway. That is, in our view, the more relevant fact about pigeon character.

Where we cannot fully restore function, we assess quality of life honestly. Some birds thrive in managed environments. We maintain relationships with permanent sanctuary placements for birds who cannot safely return to the street.

🪶
Intake is free

The city created these conditions. We do not charge the birds for its mistakes. All rehabilitation is provided at no cost to the finder.

📍
Three intake locations

Brooklyn, upper Manhattan, and Jackson Heights, Queens. Mail-in intake available for outer boroughs.

📋
Every case is documented

Your intake report becomes part of our injury dataset. We publish it annually. We share it with city council. Every bird counted.

🔄
If we can't repair

You decide what happens next. We will never dispose of a bird without your consent. Sanctuary placement is available.

Questions? Visit our Help Centre or write to us at repair@nycpigeoncollective.org. We are volunteers, and we will write back.

Survivor stories
Worn Wings · Case No. 001
Gerald
Canal St Bridge, Manhattan · Wing laceration, Type-4 spike strip · March 2025

"He came in on a Tuesday. Left wing, three punctures. He was still trying to fly."

Gerald was found by a union volunteer on the Canal Street overpass, where the building manager had installed a Type-4 stainless spike strip across the full length of the eastern ledge. Gerald had been roosting there for six years. He didn't know it was a ledge he wasn't allowed to use anymore.

The lacerations were cleaned. The wing was splinted with a balsa strip and medical tape. He spent eleven days in a recovery box in a Chinatown apartment. On the twelfth day, he flew to the windowsill. On the fourteenth, he was gone. He still comes back on Thursdays. We think he remembers.

✓ Repaired & returned to flight — April 2025
Worn Wings · Case No. 002
Marta
Fulton St, Brooklyn · Grain toxicosis, methyl anthranilate · July 2025

"The grain looked normal. It always does."

Methyl anthranilate is a bird repellent applied to grain to deter feeding. It is legal. It causes disorientation, respiratory distress, and in sufficient quantities, organ failure. Marta weighed 280 grams when she arrived. She should have weighed 340.

Recovery took three weeks. Electrolytes. Quiet. Seed that was exactly what it appeared to be. Marta is healthy. The loading dock is still active. The third letter is being prepared.

✓ Repaired · Case ongoing — August 2025
Worn Wings · Case No. 003
Percy
14th St–Union Square Station · Net entanglement · October 2025

"He came in tangled. He left strutting. We are not exaggerating."

Percy was found caught in a section of anti-nesting net that had come loose from the ceiling grid of the uptown 4/5/6 platform at 14th Street. He had been there since the previous evening's rush hour. He had eaten two french fries through the netting. The MTA worker cut him free and called the union hotline.

No lacerations. Minor wing bruising. Two days of rest. Three meals a day. Water that was not puddle water. He was back at 14th Street by the following Tuesday. The MTA worker left him half a bagel.

✓ Fully recovered — October 2025
