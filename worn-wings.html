We fix what
the city has broken.

Spike wounds. Leg-band necrosis. Netting entanglement. Grain laced with contraceptives. These are not accidents — they are the predictable outcome of a city that chose aesthetics over accountability. We are here to repair the damage, one bird at a time.

We can repair many, but not all, injuries.

You can start your intake here. Once we receive the bird, it takes us four to eight weeks on average to rehabilitate and return it to its territory.

Please do not offer food or water before bringing the bird in — well-meaning feeding can cause aspiration in injured birds. A cardboard box with air holes is all you need.

Our goal is to treat everything we can, but each case is different. Before submitting your intake, please note that for life-threatening emergencies, contact Wild Bird Fund NYC directly.

Repaired birds may not look like new.

We will always aim to restore full function. But a bird that has lost a toe to a constricting leg band, or bears scar tissue from a spike wound, carries that history in its body. We do not consider this a failure. We consider it evidence. These birds return to the city marked by what the city did to them — and they return anyway. Where full function cannot be restored, we assess quality of life honestly and maintain sanctuary placements for birds who cannot safely return to the street.

Rehabilitation is always free.

The city created these conditions. We do not charge the birds for its mistakes. All rehabilitation, care, and territory return is provided at no cost. We do ask for a voluntary contribution to cover feed and veterinary supplies if you are able — but intake will never be conditional on it.

If we can't rehabilitate your bird, what would you like us to do?

If the bird cannot be returned to the street, you get to decide what happens next — a permanent sanctuary placement or, in cases of irreversible suffering, a humane end. We will never make that decision without your consent.

Survivor story · No. 001
Gerald
Canal St Bridge, Manhattan · Wing laceration, Type-4 spike strip · Admitted March 2025

"He came in on a Tuesday. Left wing, three punctures. He was still trying to fly."

Gerald was found by a union volunteer on the Canal Street overpass, where the building manager had installed a Type-4 stainless spike strip across the full length of the eastern ledge. Gerald had been roosting there for six years. He didn't know it was a ledge he wasn't allowed to use anymore.

The lacerations were cleaned. The wing was splinted with a balsa strip and medical tape. He spent eleven days in a recovery box in a Chinatown apartment. On the twelfth day, he flew to the windowsill. On the fourteenth, he was gone. He still comes back on Thursdays. We think he remembers.

✓ Repaired & returned to flight — April 2025
Survivor story · No. 002
Marta
Fulton St, Brooklyn · Grain toxicosis, methyl anthranilate · Admitted July 2025

"The grain looked normal. It always does."

Methyl anthranilate is a bird repellent applied to grain to deter feeding. It is legal. It causes disorientation, respiratory distress, and in sufficient quantities, organ failure. Marta weighed 280 grams when she arrived. She should have weighed 340.

Recovery took three weeks. Electrolytes. Quiet. Seed that was exactly what it appeared to be. The volunteer who brought her in has been monitoring the Fulton Street loading dock every week since. The property management company has not responded to two letters from the union. Marta is healthy. The loading dock is still active. The third letter is being prepared.

✓ Repaired · Case ongoing — August 2025

Survivor story · No. 003
Percy
14th St–Union Square Station · Net entanglement · Admitted October 2025

"He came in tangled. He left strutting. We are not exaggerating."

Percy was found caught in a section of anti-nesting net that had come loose from the ceiling grid of the uptown 4/5/6 platform at 14th Street. He had been there, best estimates suggest, since the previous evening's rush hour. He had eaten two french fries through the netting. The MTA worker cut him free and called the union hotline.

No lacerations. Minor wing bruising. Two days of rest. Three meals a day. Water that was not puddle water. He was back at 14th Street by the following Tuesday. The MTA worker left him half a bagel.

✓ Fully recovered — October 2025
