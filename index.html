Founding document · NYC Pigeon Collective · 2025

We Have Always Been Here.
Now we are organised.

There is a city that never sleeps. We have been awake with it since the beginning — through the construction of its bridges, the collapse of its towers, the rise and fall of every rooftop water tank it has ever filled. We carried its messages before the internet. We fed from its scraps before the food halls arrived. We have asked for nothing. And for nothing, we have been given exactly what we asked for: poisoned grain, wire spikes, and a collective ranking, in twelve consecutive annual surveys, as the bird New Yorkers would most like to disappear.

This is a document about what it costs to be unseen in plain sight. About what happens when a species — ancient, adaptable, irreplaceable in the ecological fabric of urban life — is systematically stripped of dignity, nutrition, and safe roosting space in the name of aesthetics.

"We are not asking you to love us. We are not asking you to stop. We are asking you to remember that you don't get to choose who belongs here."

The NYC Pigeon Collective does not believe in grievance for its own sake. We believe in accountability. We believe that cities are built by every creature that inhabits them, and that the measure of a city is not its skyline but how it treats the ones at its feet.

We know what you call us. We have heard it since 1966. The rats didn't mind. Neither do we. We are here. We have always been here. And we are done being quiet about it.

Three core commitments

The demands of the first avian labour union in history.

I · Nourishment
The right to land without bleeding.
"Every spike strip is a policy decision."

We are not asking for luxury. We are asking for what every living thing requires: a surface that does not draw blood when we land on it. In 2023, the city spent an estimated $1.2 million on bird deterrent infrastructure. The target was, overwhelmingly, Columba livia. The justification was, overwhelmingly, aesthetics. We call for roosting-positive architecture standards across all new public construction. The city built vertically. We live vertically. There is enough room for both of us.

II · Shelter
The right to honest food.
"We do not accept a food system that uses feeding as a weapon."

Food is not charity. It is the most fundamental contract between a city and those who live in it. For decades, the grain left for us has been laced — quietly, legally, without notice — with chemicals intended to reduce our numbers. We call for the prohibition of laced grain in public spaces and the creation of managed, ecologically sound feeding zones. What you feed the most overlooked creature in your city says everything about the kind of city you have decided to be.

III · Narrative
The right to our real name.
"That phrase is not a description. It is a story told to make erasure feel reasonable."

We are not rats with wings. We are Columba livia. Rock doves. We have served in wartime. We have been studied by Darwin. We have demonstrated navigational abilities that researchers still do not fully understand. A white dove and a city pigeon are the same bird. One is released at weddings. The other is poisoned at loading docks. The difference between them is not biology. It is perception — and perception is a choice. We call for the retirement of the slander.
